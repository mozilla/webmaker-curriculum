Javascript Basics - aka Quacking Javascript

Learners get familiar with writing Javascript in this fun, five-part module. Together we discover the foundations of the text coding by being creative and making our own web pages.

    Teacher's Notes

    Here are some notes for teachers of this course. Please add to them by making comment in the discussion thread for this course here.

    Building Engagement

    This course has a focus on trying to engage students who may not be immediately drawn to computer coding. The process of building engagement takes several forms including:

    • Linking with real world activities
    • Use of popular culture
    • Use of games (offline and online)
    • Use of silly characters, fruits and animals

    These techniques may work for the people you are delivering the course to or you may feel they are not appropriate. Please feel free to adapt the course in any way you wish to make it.

    You can also do quick quizzes with the participants to see what bit they like and what bit left them cold. Please do feed any of this back to the Mozilla Teach the Web discussion group here.

    Drama Games

    Drama games are an effective way to break ice, encourage creative thinking and increase the connections between participants. While it can seem like a bit of a risk I would strongly encourage their use in both community and more formal settings.

    Drama games are not explicitly included in-line for each lesson. Instead there is list of ones that you may find useful here. You can probably see how they could be linked to specific lessons. Word games can be a useful one for groups where it is not appropriate to be moving around.

    Adverb Exit

    This game is a good icebreaker to get people thinking and lively.

    Someone picks a letter - A, B, C etc.

    Then someone else has to leave the room in a way that matches the letter.

    ie, They choose an adverb that begins with that letter.

    A - Anxiously, C - Cheerfully, M - Messily

    I went to market

    AKA Hypochondriac

    Aim To memorise an ever-expanding list, in sequence.

    How to play: A straightforward memory game that requires you to memorise an ever-expanding list. You can play in a large group or with just two people. Player 1 starts off: "I went to market and I bought ... a loaf of bread." Player 2 continues, adding their own item to the list: "I went to market and bought a loaf of bread and a bunch of bananas." Player 3: "I went to market and I bought a loaf of bread, a bunch of bananas and some fish." And so on. Items must be repeated in the correct order. If someone makes a mistake, they are out. Last one standing wins.

    If you don’t like I went to market then customise your list to suit the company. "I went to a party and I ate...", "I went to hospital because ..."

    Word Association

    The tours of Katy Perry are always a bit crazy with different names, dances, animals, characters, outfits and concepts they include:

    • Hello Katy Tour
    • California Dreams Tour
    • Prismatic World Tour

    Can you think of a crazy, colourful name for a new tour. It could include fruit, colours, animals, types of dancing, shapes, names.

    Go around the room and do word association make sure the tour has at least two words in it.

    • Red Lemons Tour
    • Fish Biscuits Tour
    • Lion Twist Tour, etc, etc.

    At the end of the game, let’s see what ones we can remember and write them down.

    Hack the Robot Dance

    Another Mozilla favourite is the How to Hack the Robot Dance activity. This is a silly and fun way to introduce the programmming concept of proceedural, sequential programming.

    Each thing that is done as part of the dance is an instruction. This can link to the need for exact instructions, the definition of algorithms etc.

    In our course this links to the way we create web pages. It is done line by line and we need to make sure our instructions are exact and in the right order.

    Round Singing

    A round is a musical composition in which a minimum of three voices sing exactly the same melody but with each voice beginning at different times so that different parts of the melody coincide but nevertheless fit harmoniously together.

    Try out some round singing with three parts like Frere Jacques.

    And then maybe try one out with four parts like Row your boat.

    This activity can be a fun way to introduce the subject of loops in programming.

    Ground Hog Day Loop / Forum Theatre

    Watch the Ground Hog day trailer and share the core concept.

    The following game is a simplified version of forum theatre. You need a joker/ facilitator who sets up the action and sets up a scenario and two or more actors who act it out. Each actor gets their first instructions from the facilitator. And is told to improvise

    When the action gets stuck the facilitator shouts Freeze and the actor go limp and look like they have falled asleep. The facilitator tell the actors to go get instructions on what to do differently from their "Brains". The brains are a small group of other students who tell the actors what to do next to change the scenario.

    You have a limited number of characters. Each character has to start the scene in a particular way. And each character has goal that they have to make happen.

    As a facilitator you can should start the scene with characters with different end conditions or goals that cannot be resolved. Later you should alter the conditions so that both end conditions can be met and the scene can be resolved.

    An example: There is an situation where a parent is about to see a teenager go out for the night.

    The teenager wants to stay out all night at a friends house but doesn’t want to tell the parent where they are going. You can’t leave until this is resolved.

    The parent doesn’t want their child to stay out past 10 o’clock and wants to know where they will be. You won’t let the your child leave without agreeing.

    The facilitator should start and stop the action and send the actors back to their brains to try out different scenarios. If you are doing your job right each actor and brain should be able try different things out but not be able to resolve the situation.

    When it seems appropriate, the facilitator should reshape the goals of the actors slightly to allow a resolution. Tell the child - "You will come back but not before 12. But you must fight to stay out later."

    Tell the parent - "You will let your child go out till 12. But you must fight to get them to come back earlier."

    Recap what happened with the group. Why did the loop keep happening? How was it resolved in the end?

    What about Ground Hog Day. What was the end condition? The main character had to give up his selfishness.
